Hypothesis 3

Historic redlining translates to modern-day environmental disparities.

We hypothesize that neighborhoods originally designated with lower HOLC grades (C and D) maintain higher concentrations of poverty and BIPOC communities today, while simultaneously experiencing greater modern-day heat disparities than communities graded A or B.

Figure 1

Established in 1933 as part of the New Deal, the Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) created "Residential Security" maps for over 200 growing cities, grading neighborhoods from A ("Best") to D ("Hazardous") based largely on racial demographics. These maps institutionalized the practice of redlining, cutting off lower-graded BIPOC communities from investment and systematically denying them mortgages for decades.

Segmenting California's modern census block groups by their historical 1930s HOLC grades reveals how redlining translates today to environmental disparities. In looking at neighborhoods statewide graded A ("Best") and B ("Still Desirable"), the data points cluster tightly in the bottom-left quadrant: these communities remain overwhelmingly white, low-poverty, and visually dominated by cool blue hues, indicating temperatures well below their county averages. A dramatic shift occurs as we cross into historical C ("Declining") and D ("Hazardous") zones. Here, the data points migrate aggressively upward and to the right, showing a clear modern-day consolidation of high-poverty and BIPOC communities.

Filtering the data by county reveals how local geography interacts with this historical legacy. Los Angeles County perfectly mirrors the statewide pattern: as the POC population and poverty rates climb in historical D zones, the color palette shifts aggressively to orange and deep red, exposing a severe urban heat island effect born from a century of concrete over-investment and canopy neglect.

However, filtering for San Francisco County reveals an intriguing anomaly: despite possessing clear demographic disparities in its C and D zones, almost the entire county shows cool blue tones. This uniform cooling is a geographic artifact of San Francisco's unique microclimate, and the city's tight maritime peninsula, dense marine layer, and powerful coastal wind currents heavily suppress heat spikes. While San Francisco's physical temperature is stabilized by the Pacific Ocean, the stark concentration of poverty and BIPOC populations within its historical C and D zones proves that the socioeconomic scars of redlining remain fully intact, even where nature masks the environmental disparity.
