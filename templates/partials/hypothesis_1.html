Hypothesis 1

More Children, Less Shade

We hypothesize that California neighborhoods with higher child populations have measurably less tree canopy, and that this pattern holds across the state's largest cities once income is accounted for.

Plotting every California census block group against the statewide canopy average (see the "Statewide Scatter" tab), the trend line slopes downward: neighborhoods where children make up a larger share of the population consistently fall below the average. This held even after accounting for rural counties like Shasta and Tuolumne, where natural forest cover can inflate canopy numbers without reflecting urban investment.

Breaking the data into California's five largest urban areas ("Faceted by City (Raw Canopy)" tab) revealed something unexpected. LA, Riverside, and Sacramento all confirm the statewide pattern, more children, less canopy. But San Diego and San Francisco appear to break it, showing flat or positive slopes.

Switching to the poverty-filtered tab, which only shows high-poverty block groups (≥20% below the federal line): San Diego's positive slope collapses. Its apparent exception was an artifact of wealthy neighborhoods, filtering to poverty removes the noise. San Francisco's slope softens but persists, hinting at something more structural.

Raw canopy has a blind spot: it measures trees, not equity. The Tree Equity Score (TES) folds in income, urban heat, health outcomes, and density alongside canopy, a neighborhood can have adequate trees and still score low if conditions make shade more critical there. Switching to the TES tab, the exceptions disappear: all five cities show a negative relationship between child share and tree equity. This is the key finding: the tree equity gap for children is universal across California's urban areas.

Mapping the three largest cities ("3-City Map" tab) reveals how differently the deficit plays out geographically. Los Angeles is overwhelmingly red in its urban core, deficit concentrated exactly where children are most dense, with green appearing only at the wealthy hillside periphery or low-density outer areas. San Diego shows more variation, mature street tree networks inner-city, lagging suburban fringe. San Francisco shows a compressed range with few extreme outliers, but equity gaps persist in the Tenderloin and Bayview.

Finding: California's tree equity gap for children is structural and citywide. Raw canopy can mislead, the San Diego and San Francisco exceptions disappear once controlling for income or measuring equity directly. Los Angeles's deficit runs deepest. Where a child lives determines not just whether they have shade, but whether their neighborhood is on the path to getting it.
