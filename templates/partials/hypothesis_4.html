Hypothesis 4

Socioeconomically Disadvantaged Neighborhoods Have Lower Tree Equity Scores

This section explores whether socioeconomically disadvantaged communities consistently experience lower Tree Equity Scores and identifies the environmental factors associated with these disparities. The prototype combines three coordinated, interactive visualizations that allow users to investigate the relationship between socioeconomic disadvantage, geographic location, and environmental conditions. Together, these views show that disadvantaged communities generally have lower Tree Equity Scores, while also highlighting where these disparities occur and how they relate to tree canopy and urban heat.

The section is structured as a top-to-bottom narrative to guide users from a broad geographic overview to progressively deeper analysis. The first view presents two synchronized county-level choropleth maps comparing average Tree Equity Score and Priority Index, providing a statewide perspective and allowing users to identify regions requiring the greatest attention. Selecting a county filters the remaining visualizations, enabling seamless drill-down exploration. (not yet implemented) The second view is an interactive scatterplot relating tree canopy to temperature difference, with points colored by a user-selected metric such as Tree Equity Score or Priority Index. This allows users to investigate the environmental conditions associated with disadvantaged communities and identify areas that experience both limited canopy and elevated temperatures. Additionally, axis averages split the plot into quadrants, allowing users to draw further conclusions based on relative quadrant position. Finally, the section concludes with a distribution plot comparing Tree Equity Scores between disadvantaged and non-disadvantaged communities, summarizing the overall statewide disparity and reinforcing the trends observed in the previous visualizations.

This layout follows the natural workflow of exploratory analysis: users first identify where disparities exist, then explore why they exist through environmental relationships, and finally validate those observations with a statewide comparison of Tree Equity Score distributions. This hopefully creates a clear narrative while encouraging interaction through coordinated filters and cross-highlighting, allowing users to move fluidly between statewide patterns and localized investigations.
