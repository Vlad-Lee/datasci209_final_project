Introduction

Why Tree Equity Matters

Access to shade is a health imperative, and it is not distributed evenly across California. This project maps where the gaps are widest, and why.

Higher tree canopy is linked to cooler summer temperatures, lower stress, and better respiratory health, and it meaningfully reduces heat-related deaths, which disproportionately affect vulnerable populations. Yet canopy cover is not distributed evenly across California, and the gaps tend to follow the same lines as income, race, and density that shape other environmental burdens.

This project builds an interactive visual of California's tree canopy, identifying where the need for shade is greatest. It's built for policymakers, city planners, and environmental activists deciding where to plant next, so they can look up Tree Equity Scores, compare them across cities, and trace the patterns behind them.

We test four hypotheses: that neighborhoods with more children have less canopy, that more canopy means cooler blocks, that areas redlined by 1930s HOLC grades still run hotter and poorer today, and that socioeconomically disadvantaged neighborhoods score lower on tree equity overall. Together, they point to one throughline: the canopy gap consistently lines up with disadvantage.
