Hypothesis 2

Do neighborhoods with more tree canopy actually run cooler?

Every point in this section is a California census block group. Scroll down to see what the data shows.

Figure 1

The leafier the block, the cooler it runs.

Average temperature sits about 1.8°C above the city average where there is almost no canopy, and drops to about 5.4°C below it in the leafiest blocks. The trend is steady across all 24,000+ California block groups. Canopy on its own only explains about 9% of the variation, so it is one real factor among several, not the whole story.

So why does canopy cool a block? It comes down to shade, and specifically shade from trees and plants rather than from buildings. The chart below plots how much of each block's shade comes from vegetation against how hot it runs. Use the tabs to change the time of day and watch how the pattern shifts.

Temperature is measured against each city's own average, not in absolute degrees, so compare blocks within a city rather than across cities.

Figure 2

Explore it yourself. Click through the times of day, and hover any point to read that block's exact shade and temperature.

The relationship is steepest at midday, when the sun is high and tree shade does the most work, and it flattens out by evening. Either way the direction holds: more vegetation shade, cooler blocks. That sets up the next question, which is who actually lives in the hottest blocks.
